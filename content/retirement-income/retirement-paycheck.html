Most people spend 30 or 40 years building up a nest egg. Then retirement arrives and a new question takes over: how do I turn what I saved into a paycheck that lasts? There is no single right answer, but there is a logical way to think it through. This guide walks you through the four main moving parts—Social Security timing, a bucket strategy, monthly withdrawal mechanics, and tax-smart sequencing—so you can see how they fit together.

When should I claim Social Security?

Social Security is often the backbone of a retirement income plan because it is guaranteed for life and adjusts for inflation each year. The age you start collecting, however, makes a significant difference in the monthly amount you receive.

Claiming Age What It Means Best Fits When…
Age 62
(earliest)		 Benefit is permanently reduced—often by 25–30% compared to your full amount Health concerns, limited savings, or you need income right away
Full Retirement Age
(66–67, depending on birth year)		 You receive your full calculated benefit with no reduction You have stopped working but do not need income immediately
Age 70
(latest for credits)		 Benefit grows roughly 8% for each year you wait past full retirement age Good health, other income sources to bridge the gap, and concern about outliving money
Key Takeaway

Delaying from 62 to 70 can increase your monthly benefit by 75% or more, depending on your birth year. For someone worried about running out of money in their 80s or 90s, a higher lifetime benefit may offer meaningful peace of mind. That said, the right timing depends on your health, your spouse’s situation, and what other income you have in the meantime.

If you are married, coordinating with your spouse’s benefit can matter just as much as your own claiming age. A surviving spouse often inherits the higher of the two benefits, so the decision one partner makes can shape the other’s financial security for decades.

What is the bucket strategy for retirement income?

One of the most practical frameworks for managing retirement savings is dividing your money into three “buckets,” each serving a different purpose and time horizon. This approach gives you cash on hand for near-term needs while keeping longer-term money invested and growing.

Bucket 1 — Cash 1–2 Years of Expenses
  • High-yield savings account
  • Money market account
  • Short-term CDs

Purpose: Cover living expenses now, no selling required

Bucket 2 — Short-Term Years 3–7
  • Bond funds or bond ladders
  • Dividend-paying investments
  • Lower-volatility balanced funds

Purpose: Refill Bucket 1 over time

Bucket 3 — Long-Term 8+ Years Out
  • Stock funds (domestic & international)
  • Real estate investment trusts
  • Growth-oriented holdings

Purpose: Outpace inflation over the long run

The core benefit of this setup is psychological as much as financial. When markets drop sharply, you know your next two years of living expenses are sitting safely in Bucket 1. You do not have to sell stocks at a bad time. You simply draw from cash while waiting for markets to recover before refilling.

Important Note

The bucket strategy is a framework, not a guarantee of any particular outcome. How much you need in each bucket depends on your spending, your other income sources like Social Security or a pension, and your comfort with market fluctuation.

How do I set up a reliable monthly withdrawal?

Knowing which accounts hold your money is one thing. Getting a consistent, predictable deposit into your checking account each month is another. A few practical steps can make this feel much more like a paycheck and much less like a guessing game.

  1. Calculate your monthly income gap. Add up what Social Security, pensions, or other guaranteed income covers. Whatever remains after your expected monthly expenses is the gap your savings need to fill.
  2. Set a sustainable withdrawal rate. Many planners suggest starting around 4% of your portfolio per year as a rough starting point, though the right rate depends on your age, health, and other factors. Divide that annual amount by 12 to find your monthly draw.
  3. Automate a transfer from Bucket 1. Set a recurring monthly transfer from your high-yield savings or money market account into your checking account. This mimics a paycheck and removes the temptation to over-withdraw in good markets.
  4. Review and refill quarterly. Every three months, check whether Bucket 1 needs topping off from Bucket 2, and whether Bucket 2 needs rebalancing from Bucket 3. Markets do the heavy lifting; you just maintain the system.
  5. Adjust annually. At the start of each year, revisit your spending, your portfolio balance, and any income changes. Small adjustments early are much easier than large corrections later.

What are Required Minimum Distributions and when do they start?

If you have money in a traditional IRA, 401(k), or similar pre-tax retirement account, the IRS requires you to begin taking withdrawals at a certain age. These are called Required Minimum Distributions, or RMDs.

Under current rules, RMDs generally begin at age 73 for most people (age 75 for those born in 1960 or later). The IRS calculates your minimum based on your account balance at the end of the prior year and a life-expectancy factor from their tables. Miss a required withdrawal and the penalty can be steep—historically as high as 50% of the amount you were supposed to take, though recent legislation has reduced it in some cases.

Why This Matters for Your Income Plan

RMDs are not optional, so they need to be built into your withdrawal plan from the start. In some years, your required distribution may be larger than what you actually need to spend. That means you may owe taxes on income you did not choose to take. Planning ahead—sometimes starting years before RMDs kick in—can help reduce this burden.

Strategies to manage RMDs

  • Roth conversions in your 60s: Moving pre-tax money into a Roth IRA before RMDs start reduces the size of your taxable accounts and your future required withdrawals. Taxes are paid on conversion, but future Roth growth and withdrawals are typically tax-free.
  • Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs): If you are 70½ or older and charitably inclined, you may be able to send your RMD directly to a qualifying charity. The amount can count toward your required distribution without being included in taxable income.
  • Spreading withdrawals over time: Rather than waiting until the deadline, some people begin taking voluntary distributions from pre-tax accounts in their late 60s to “level out” taxable income across years and potentially reduce Medicare premium surcharges.

Should I withdraw from my IRA or my Roth first?

Most retirees have money spread across two or three different types of accounts, each taxed differently. The order in which you draw them down—often called tax sequencing or withdrawal order—can make a meaningful difference in how long your money lasts.

Account Type How It Is Taxed General Guidance
Taxable brokerage accounts Capital gains rates on growth; dividends taxed annually Often drawn first, especially for low-taxed long-term gains
Traditional IRA / 401(k) (pre-tax) Ordinary income tax on every dollar withdrawn Use strategically; RMDs will force withdrawals after age 73
Roth IRA / Roth 401(k) Withdrawals typically tax-free if account is 5+ years old Often preserved longest; powerful for managing tax brackets in high-income years

A common approach is to draw from taxable accounts first, then pre-tax accounts, and preserve Roth money for last. But this is a general guideline, not a rule. In years when your taxable income is lower—perhaps because you are not yet receiving Social Security or pension income—it may make sense to pull from your traditional IRA and pay taxes at a lower rate, effectively reducing the account before RMDs force larger distributions later.

A Word on Annuities

Some retirees use a portion of their savings to purchase an annuity, which can provide a guaranteed stream of income for life. Those guarantees are subject to the insurance company’s claims-paying ability. Annuities vary widely in structure and cost, so it is worth understanding what you are buying before committing. They may fit into a broader income plan, but they are rarely the entire plan.

How do all these pieces work together?

Building a retirement paycheck is not one decision—it is several decisions that interact with each other. Here is a simple way to think about the sequence:

  • Start with guaranteed income. Know what Social Security and any pensions will provide and when. This is your floor.
  • Size your income gap. Subtract your guaranteed income from your expected monthly spending. Your savings need to cover the rest.
  • Organize savings into buckets. Align your money by time horizon so you have cash for the near term and growth potential for the long term.
  • Automate your monthly draw. A regular, predictable transfer from your cash bucket to your checking account removes emotion from the equation.
  • Plan around RMDs and taxes. Know when distributions are required and think ahead about whether Roth conversions or voluntary early withdrawals make sense for your situation.
  • Review once a year. Life changes. So do tax laws, market conditions, and spending needs. An annual check-in keeps the plan current.

No single piece of this works perfectly in isolation. Social Security timing affects how much you need from savings. Your bucket sizes depend on your spending gap. Your tax strategy depends on which accounts you have and how large they are. A good retirement income plan treats all of these as one connected system, not separate problems to solve one at a time.
