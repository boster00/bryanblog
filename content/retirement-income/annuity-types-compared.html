If you are thinking about protecting your retirement income, you have likely come across three types of products that all use the word "annuity." They sound similar, but they work quite differently. This page explains each one in plain language so you can have a more informed conversation with a financial professional.

What is a fixed annuity, and how does it work?

A fixed annuity is a contract with an insurance company. You put in a lump sum, and the company credits your account with a set interest rate for a specific period—often one to five years. When that term ends, the rate is reset based on current conditions.

Think of it as similar to a bank certificate of deposit (CD), but issued by an insurance company instead of a bank. Your balance grows at a guaranteed rate during the term. You generally cannot lose money due to market drops.

  • Growth is steady and predictable for the length of the term
  • No market exposure—your principal is not invested in stocks or bonds
  • Withdrawals before a certain period may trigger surrender charges
  • Earnings are tax-deferred until you take money out
  • Any guarantees are subject to the insurance company’s claims-paying ability

A fixed annuity may fit someone who wants a predictable, conservative place to let savings grow while keeping them out of the market.

What is a fixed indexed annuity, and what makes it different?

A fixed indexed annuity also comes with a floor that protects you from losing principal due to market declines. The difference is how interest is calculated. Instead of a fixed rate, the interest credited to your account is linked—in part—to the performance of a market index, such as a broad stock index.

If the index goes up, you may receive a portion of that gain, up to a cap or subject to a participation rate set in your contract. If the index goes down, you typically do not lose money due to market performance—but you also may not earn anything that year.

  • Potential for more growth than a traditional fixed annuity in good market years
  • You do not directly own stocks or market assets—your money is not invested in the index
  • Caps and participation rates limit how much of any index gain you receive
  • Contracts can be complex; terms vary widely from one policy to another
  • Surrender charges and other conditions apply; any guarantees are subject to the insurance company’s claims-paying ability

A fixed indexed annuity may fit someone who wants downside protection but is open to some upside potential tied to market conditions, without direct market risk.

What is an income annuity, and when does it make sense?

An income annuity—sometimes called an immediate annuity or deferred income annuity, depending on when payments begin—works differently from the two types above. Rather than growing a balance, an income annuity converts a lump sum into a stream of regular payments.

You hand a sum of money to an insurance company, and in return, you receive guaranteed income payments—monthly, quarterly, or annually—for a set period or for the rest of your life, depending on how you set it up.

  • Payments are predictable and can be structured to last your entire lifetime
  • Removes the worry of outliving your money, depending on your situation
  • Once you start, you typically cannot get the lump sum back (unless a return-of-premium feature is included)
  • Less flexibility than accumulation-focused products
  • All guarantees are subject to the insurance company’s claims-paying ability

An income annuity may fit someone who wants to cover essential expenses with a reliable, predictable paycheck they cannot outlive.

What are the typical tradeoffs to keep in mind?

Each type involves a real tradeoff. Understanding it helps you ask better questions.

With a fixed annuity

You gain predictability, but your growth is capped at the declared rate. If interest rates rise significantly after you lock in, you may not benefit until the term ends. You also give up liquidity for a period of time.

With a fixed indexed annuity

You may earn more in a strong market year, but caps and participation rates mean you will not capture full market gains. Contracts can be difficult to compare. Some riders that add income features carry additional costs that reduce your potential growth.

With an income annuity

You gain the peace of mind of a lifetime paycheck, but you give up control of the lump sum. If you pass away early, payments may stop depending on how you structured the contract. Inflation can erode the purchasing power of fixed payments over time, though some contracts offer cost-of-living adjustments.

Who are these products typically not a good fit for?

These products may not be right for you if: you expect to need the full lump sum within a few years, you are in poor health and may not live long enough to benefit from lifetime income options, you need significant liquidity for large near-term expenses, or you are not comfortable with the complexity of the contract terms. These are long-term commitments, not short-term savings vehicles.

A fixed annuity is generally not a good fit if you need easy access to your money in the short term or if you want market-linked growth.

A fixed indexed annuity is generally not a good fit if you are uncomfortable with contract complexity, dislike the idea of capped gains, or need straightforward, predictable interest.

An income annuity is generally not a good fit if you may need the lump sum back, have significant health concerns that could shorten life expectancy, or if covering everyday expenses is already well-handled by other guaranteed income like Social Security or a pension.

What questions should I ask before moving forward?

  • What is my main goal—protecting savings, earning more, or creating income I cannot outlive?
  • How long am I comfortable keeping this money locked up?
  • Do I already have enough guaranteed income to cover my essential monthly expenses?
  • How does this fit alongside my Social Security, pension, or other retirement income?
  • What are the total costs, including any rider fees or surrender charges?
  • What happens to the remaining balance or payments if I pass away?

These questions do not have one right answer. The answer depends on your health, your other income, your family situation, and how you feel about risk and flexibility.
