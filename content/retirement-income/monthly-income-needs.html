One of the biggest worries people carry into retirement is a simple one: will my money last as long as I do? Before you can answer that, you need a realistic picture of how much you will actually spend each month once you stop working. That number is more personal than most guides admit — but there are proven starting points that make it easier to find yours.

What is a typical replacement rate?

Financial planners often use a rule of thumb called the income replacement rate — the percentage of your pre-retirement income you will need to maintain a similar standard of living. Most guidance places that range between 70 and 85 percent of your final working income.

Why less than 100 percent? When you retire, several large expenses often shrink or go away. You stop contributing to a retirement account. Payroll taxes drop. Work-related costs — commuting, uniforms, lunches out — largely disappear. And in many cases, your mortgage is paid off or nearly so.

70% Lower range — modest lifestyle, paid-off home, few new activities 80% Common midpoint — often used as a starting estimate 85%+ Higher range — active travel, helping family, ongoing housing costs

Quick estimate

If you currently earn $6,000 a month, the 70–85% rule suggests you may need roughly $4,200 to $5,100 a month to cover living expenses in retirement. Treat that as a starting point, not a final answer.

Keep in mind that a replacement rate is just a shortcut. Some retirees spend more in their early years — the "go-go years" of travel and activity — and then less as they slow down. Others face rising healthcare costs that push spending higher in later decades. The sections below will help you sharpen that rough estimate into something more personal.

How do I budget by category?

The clearest way to estimate your retirement income need is to go category by category — rather than just applying a single percentage to your salary. Pull up three months of recent bank and credit card statements and sort your spending into the buckets below.

Category Type What to keep in mind
Housing (mortgage or rent, taxes, insurance) Fixed May decrease if mortgage is paid off; property taxes often continue
Food & groceries Flexible Dining out may increase with more free time
Healthcare & insurance Fixed Often rises significantly; plan for premiums, copays, dental, vision
Transportation Flexible May drop without a commute; one car instead of two
Travel & leisure Flexible Often highest in early retirement; plan for the "go-go years"
Utilities Fixed Spending more time at home may raise heating, cooling, and electricity
Gifts & family support Flexible Easy to underestimate; grandchildren, weddings, emergencies
Home maintenance Fixed Budget separately — see the next section

Once you have a monthly total across these categories, compare it to your replacement-rate estimate. If the numbers are close, you have a reasonable baseline. If they differ sharply, the category-by-category figure is usually more reliable because it reflects your actual spending patterns.

Fixed expenses vs. flexible expenses

Not all expenses are equal when it comes to planning. It helps to separate your spending into two groups.

Fixed expenses

These are the costs that show up every month whether you want them to or not — housing, utilities, insurance premiums, and minimum debt payments. They are the floor of your income need. You need a reliable, predictable income stream that covers these first. Social Security, a pension, or certain guaranteed income products may help cover this floor, depending on your situation.

Flexible expenses

These are the costs you can adjust — dining, travel, subscriptions, entertainment. Having a cushion of flexible spending gives you the ability to cut back during a market downturn without missing a bill payment. Experienced retirement planners often recommend making sure your fixed expenses are fully covered by guaranteed income before you draw on savings.

Planning tip

Add up your fixed monthly expenses first. That sum is your income floor — the minimum you need to arrive each month no matter what markets do. Then add flexible expenses on top for your full income target.

What about unexpected expenses?

One of the most common planning mistakes is budgeting only for what you expect and ignoring what you don't. Two categories deserve their own line item.

Healthcare costs

Healthcare is often the largest surprise in retirement budgets. Even with Medicare, out-of-pocket costs — premiums, deductibles, copays, dental, vision, hearing, and prescription drugs — can add up to several thousand dollars a year for a typical retiree couple, and considerably more if serious illness or long-term care is involved.

If you retire before age 65, you will also need to bridge the gap to Medicare eligibility, which can mean buying private coverage at a substantial monthly cost. Build healthcare into its own category in your budget, and revisit it annually.

Home repair and maintenance

A common rule of thumb is to budget one to two percent of your home's value per year for maintenance and repairs. On a $300,000 home, that is $3,000 to $6,000 annually — or $250 to $500 a month. Roofs, HVAC systems, plumbing, and appliances do not check your retirement account balance before they fail.

A practical approach

Keep a dedicated emergency reserve — separate from your investment portfolio — that covers at least six months of fixed expenses. This buffer lets you handle surprises without disrupting your long-term income plan.

How will inflation change what I need?

Here is a number that surprises many people: if you retire at 65, you may be in retirement for 25 years or more. That is a long time for prices to rise.

At a modest 3% annual inflation rate — which is roughly the long-run historical average for consumer prices — the purchasing power of a fixed dollar amount erodes meaningfully over decades. What costs $4,000 a month today may feel like it costs far more to maintain the same lifestyle 20 years from now.

This is why income sources that include a cost-of-living adjustment (COLA) — like Social Security — are particularly valuable. For income from other sources that are fixed in dollar terms, you may want to plan for your portfolio or savings to help make up the difference over time. Certain income products may also offer inflation protection features, depending on your situation.

Healthcare costs, in particular, have historically risen faster than general inflation. That is an additional reason to treat healthcare as its own planning category rather than folding it into a broad percentage.

Matching income sources to your number

Once you have a clearer monthly target, the next step is to map your expected income sources against it. Most people have some combination of:

  • Social Security — a government benefit with a built-in cost-of-living adjustment; the amount depends on your earnings history and the age at which you claim
  • Pension income — if you have a defined-benefit pension from an employer, this may cover a substantial portion of your fixed expenses
  • Withdrawals from savings — IRAs, 401(k)s, and other accounts can supplement guaranteed income, though withdrawals are subject to market performance and tax treatment
  • Part-time work — many retirees work part-time in early retirement, which can ease the draw on savings
  • Guaranteed income products — certain products may provide a steady income stream for life, subject to the insurance company's claims-paying ability and the terms of the contract

The goal is to make sure your fixed expenses are covered by predictable, reliable income — and that you have flexibility in the rest of your plan to handle surprises, inflation, and changes in spending over time.

Your next step

You now have the building blocks: a replacement-rate estimate, a category-by-category budget, a sense of fixed versus flexible expenses, a plan for surprises, and an understanding of what inflation does over 25 years.

But knowing the framework and running the numbers for your specific situation are two different things. The income you will need depends on when you plan to retire, what you already have saved, how your Social Security benefit is likely to be calculated, and a dozen other details that are unique to you.

A retirement income review with a qualified advisor can help you put those pieces together into a written income plan — one that accounts for your fixed costs, your lifestyle goals, and the long-term impact of inflation. There is no obligation involved in asking for a review, and many people find that simply having a clear picture reduces the anxiety considerably.
