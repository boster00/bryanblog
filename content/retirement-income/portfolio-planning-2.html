Why Having a Plan Matters More in Retirement Than Before

During your working years, a bad stretch in the market was something you could wait out. You had a paycheck coming in, so you did not have to sell investments at a low point just to cover groceries or the electric bill.

Retirement changes that. Once you start drawing money from your savings instead of adding to it, how you take money out—and when—becomes just as important as how much you saved in the first place.

A retirement income plan is simply a clear picture of where each month’s spending will come from and what you do when conditions change. Without one, it is easy to make decisions under pressure: selling investments during a down market, holding too much in cash out of worry, or withdrawing too fast early on.

The core idea: You want a layer of income that covers your basics regardless of what the market does, plus growth money that works over time, plus some cash you can reach without selling anything.

The Three Legs: Social Security, Your Savings, and Guaranteed Income Sources

Most retirement income plans draw from three general pools. Each one plays a different role, and the mix that makes sense for you depends on your situation.

Social Security

For most people, Social Security is the most reliable source of income in retirement. It adjusts for inflation over time and lasts as long as you live. One of the biggest levers you have is when you claim. Claiming later—up to age 70—results in a larger monthly amount. If you are in good health, delaying can provide meaningfully more income over a long retirement. This decision is worth careful thought because it is hard to undo.

Your Personal Savings (IRAs, 401(k)s, and Taxable Accounts)

Your savings fill the gap between what Social Security pays and what you actually need to spend. They also serve as a reserve for unexpected costs and a potential source of money to pass on. Because these accounts need to last decades, they generally still need some exposure to growth-oriented investments even in retirement—though how much depends on your timeline and comfort level.

Guaranteed Income Sources Beyond Social Security

Some people have a pension from an employer or union. Others may consider converting a portion of savings into a stream of guaranteed income through a contract with an insurance company. Products of this kind come with trade-offs—more on that in Section 4—and vary widely in their terms. Any guaranteed income from a private source is only as reliable as the financial strength of the company providing it.

Annuity income guarantees are subject to the claims-paying ability of the issuing insurance company. Before entering into any contract, review the company’s financial strength ratings from independent rating agencies.

Understanding the Trade-Offs: Liquidity, Cost, and Growth Potential

Every income source you can choose from involves trade-offs. There is no option that is unlimited in every direction at once. Knowing what you are giving up—and what you are gaining—makes it easier to build something you can stick with.

Liquidity

Some income sources lock up your money for years or permanently. Cash accounts and publicly traded investments can be accessed quickly. Annuities and certain contracts often carry surrender periods or penalties for early withdrawal.

Costs and Fees

All financial products carry costs, though they vary significantly. Investment funds charge ongoing expense ratios. Insurance-based products often have layers of fees built into their pricing. Costs reduce the net benefit you receive and should be understood before you commit.

Giving Up Some Upside

Guaranteed income is predictable, which is valuable. But that predictability usually means you participate less—or not at all—in strong market gains. Growth assets do the opposite: they can increase purchasing power over time, but their value fluctuates.

The question is not which type of asset is “best.” It is how much of each to hold given your specific income needs, timeline, and comfort with uncertainty.

The Timing Problem: Why Market Drops Early in Retirement Hit Harder

One of the most important and least understood retirement risks is called sequence-of-returns risk. It is not about how markets perform on average over many years. It is about the order in which gains and losses occur.

If a significant market decline happens in the first few years after you start drawing from your savings, the math works against you in a way it did not during your working years:

  • You sell shares at low prices to cover living expenses.
  • Those shares are gone and cannot participate in any future recovery.
  • Your remaining balance is smaller, so when markets do recover, you gain back less in absolute terms.

How Much Is Enough? Sizing Your Income Layers Without a Magic Number

There is no single right answer for how much guaranteed income, growth investment, or cash reserve you should hold. Anyone who tells you otherwise is oversimplifying. That said, a few practical approaches can help you think through reasonable ranges.

Start by estimating your fixed, non-negotiable monthly costs: housing, utilities, food, insurance, and medications. This is the floor your guaranteed income should aim to cover, at minimum.

Travel, gifts, hobbies, and extra spending can often be reduced temporarily if needed. These are better funded from your investment portfolio or from flexible income sources.

Many people underestimate how long they will live. Prices also rise over time. A plan that looks adequate today may fall short in 20 years if it does not account for both of these factors.

A useful starting point is to write down your essential monthly expenses and then list your known income sources—Social Security, any pensions, or other guaranteed income. If those sources cover your essentials, your investment portfolio can focus on growth and flexibility rather than survival. If there is a gap, that gap becomes the problem your planning needs to solve.

No calculator replaces your specific situation. General rules of thumb for withdrawal rates and income sizing are widely discussed and can be a starting point, but they carry assumptions about markets, lifespan, and spending that may not match yours.

How to Think About Withdrawals When Markets Are Up or Down

Once you have your income layers in place, you still face ongoing decisions: which account to pull from, and in what order. The basic logic is straightforward, even if the details get complicated.

In most conditions

Draw first from your guaranteed income and cash reserves to cover regular living expenses. Let your growth investments continue working. Rebalance periodically to keep your mix aligned with your goals.

When markets are down significantly

Avoid selling growth investments if you can help it. This is exactly why you hold cash reserves and have guaranteed income covering your basics. If you must draw from investments, take only what you need and look at which holdings have held up best rather than selling indiscriminately.

When markets are up

A strong market period can be a good time to rebalance—trimming some growth holdings and refilling your cash reserves. It may also be worth reviewing whether you are on track or could afford to be a bit more conservative.

Tax considerations shape order, too

Which account you draw from first has tax consequences. Traditional IRA and 401(k) withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income. Roth accounts are generally tax-free in retirement. Taxable investment accounts have their own rules. The order of withdrawals is worth discussing with an advisor who knows your full picture.

Required minimum distributions (RMDs) from certain accounts must begin at a specific age under current tax law. Ignoring them can result in significant tax penalties. Make sure your plan accounts for RMD timing.

Your Next-Steps Checklist

You do not have to figure all of this out at once. Start with these practical steps and build from there.

  • Know your Social Security estimate. Create a free account at SSA.gov to see your projected benefit at different claiming ages. Understand how delaying changes your monthly amount.
  • Write down your monthly essential expenses. Be specific. Include housing, utilities, food, health insurance, prescriptions, and transportation. This number is your income floor.
  • List all of your income sources. Include Social Security, any pensions, part-time work, rental income, and any guaranteed income contracts you already hold.
  • Identify your income gap. Subtract your known monthly income from your monthly essential expenses. If there is a gap, that is the amount your savings or other sources need to fill.
  • Check your cash cushion. Do you have one to two years of living expenses in accessible, stable accounts? If not, consider building that up before or early in retirement.
  • Review your investment mix. Does the risk level in your portfolio match your timeline and your need to draw income? A mix that worked at 45 may not be right at 65.
  • Understand your required minimum distributions. If you have traditional retirement accounts, find out when your RMDs begin and what that means for your taxable income in retirement.
  • Talk to someone who can see the whole picture. A retirement income review with a qualified advisor can help you connect all these pieces and spot gaps you might have missed working through it on your own.
