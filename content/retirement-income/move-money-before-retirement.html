If retirement is a few years away, you may be asking yourself: should I pull back from the stock market, or stay put? It is one of the most common questions people face in their 50s and 60s—and the answer is almost never “all or nothing.”

Why do people want to move everything to cash before retirement?

After watching a market drop cut a retirement account balance by thousands of dollars in a matter of weeks, the idea of moving to cash feels like safety. That feeling is completely understandable. But the fear of loss can lead to a different kind of loss—one that is quieter and slower, but just as real.

When money sits in cash or very low-yield accounts, it often does not keep up with inflation. Over a 20- or 30-year retirement, the purchasing power of those dollars can shrink meaningfully. A dollar that buys a full bag of groceries today may only buy part of one a decade from now.

A key idea: Risk in retirement is not just about market drops. It also includes the risk of your money losing ground to rising prices over time. Both risks deserve attention.

What happens if I stay 100% in stocks up to retirement?

On the other end of the spectrum, staying fully invested in stocks right up to the day you retire carries its own serious risk. Financial planners often call this sequence-of-returns risk—a plain-English way of saying that the timing of a bad year matters as much as the size of it.

If the market drops sharply in the first few years of retirement, and you are drawing income from your portfolio at the same time, you are selling shares while they are down. That combination—losses plus withdrawals—can permanently reduce how long your money lasts, even if the market eventually recovers. Recovering from a big early loss is much harder once you are in the spending phase.

What is a glide path, and how does it work?

A glide path is simply the idea of gradually reducing your exposure to stocks as you get closer to—and then into—retirement. Think of it like a plane coming in for a landing: you do not drop straight down, and you do not stay at cruising altitude until the last second. You ease down in a controlled way.

In practice, this means slowly shifting a portion of your portfolio from assets that tend to grow (like stocks) toward assets that tend to be steadier (like bonds or other income-oriented holdings) over a period of years. The transition is gradual, not a single sudden move.

How far in advance should I start shifting my portfolio?

Most financial professionals suggest that the transition should begin several years before retirement—often somewhere in the range of five to ten years out. Starting early means each adjustment is small, which avoids the risk of making one large move at the wrong time.

The pace and extent of the shift depends on a number of personal factors, including:

  • How long you expect your retirement to last (many people plan for 25–30 years or more)
  • Whether you have other income sources, such as a pension or Social Security, that reduce how much you need to draw from your portfolio
  • Your comfort level with seeing your balance fluctuate—honestly, not just in theory
  • Other assets, debts, or financial obligations that affect your situation

There is no single right answer that fits everyone. Someone with a reliable pension and modest expenses may be comfortable keeping more in stocks. Someone who depends entirely on their portfolio for income may want a steadier mix earlier.

Should I use any guaranteed income in my retirement plan?

Some people find it helpful to set aside a portion of their savings in a way that produces predictable income—money they can count on regardless of what the market does. This can make it easier to leave the rest of their portfolio invested for growth, because day-to-day spending is covered.

Certain insurance products—such as annuities—are designed to provide this kind of steady income. Any guarantees provided by an annuity are subject to the insurance company’s claims-paying ability. Whether a product like this makes sense depends heavily on your specific situation, costs, and goals. It is not the right choice for everyone, and it should always be weighed alongside other options.

What does a sensible pre-retirement shift generally look like?

While specific numbers depend on your situation, a general pattern that many advisors suggest follows a few common principles:

  • Begin gradually, not all at once. Small adjustments over time are easier to manage than one large move.
  • Keep some growth-oriented assets even in retirement. A retirement that lasts 25 years still needs some long-term growth to stay ahead of inflation.
  • Think in “buckets.” Near-term spending needs more stability; money you will not touch for 10–15 years can still take on more risk.
  • Review the plan regularly. Life changes—health, family, expenses—and your mix may need to change with it.

The goal is not to eliminate risk. The goal is to have the right kinds of risk for each part of your retirement timeline.

What questions should I ask before making any changes?

Before moving money around, it helps to think through a few basic questions:

  • If the market dropped 20% tomorrow, how would that affect my actual retirement plans—not just my feelings?
  • Do I have enough in stable, accessible accounts to cover two to three years of living expenses without selling stocks?
  • Am I making this decision based on a clear plan, or based on recent headlines?
  • Have I looked at the tax consequences of any large moves?

If you are within ten years of retirement and not sure whether your current mix still fits your situation, a retirement income review can help bring some clarity. A review looks at your full picture—what you have, what you will need, and how the two connect—so that any adjustments you make are based on a plan rather than worry. There is no obligation, and no pressure. It is simply a conversation to help you feel more confident about where you are headed.
