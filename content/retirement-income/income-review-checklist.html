A retirement income review is a working conversation. The more you bring to it, the more useful it becomes. You do not need to have everything sorted out ahead of time. You just need the right pieces of paper and a few things to think through. This guide walks you through both.

What documents should I bring?

You do not need a filing cabinet. A handful of key documents gives your advisor enough to work with. If you cannot find something, bring what you have and note what is missing.

Income and benefit statements

  • Social Security statement — your most recent letter or the estimate from ssa.gov. It shows your benefit at different claiming ages.
  • Pension documents — the summary plan description and any benefit estimate. If you have a choice between a lump sum and monthly payments, bring that paperwork too.
  • Any existing annuity contracts — the face page and most recent statement. Guarantees on these contracts are subject to the insurance company’s claims-paying ability.

Savings and investment accounts

  • Most recent statements for your IRA, 401(k), 403(b), or any other retirement accounts — including balances and investment holdings.
  • Brokerage or bank statements for savings you hold outside of retirement accounts.

Tax and expense information

  • Last year’s federal tax return — the first two pages are enough. This helps estimate your tax situation in retirement.
  • A rough monthly expense estimate — what you spend now, and what you expect to spend in retirement. A back-of-the-envelope number is fine. Bills, mortgage or rent, and recurring costs are a good start.

What questions should I think through beforehand?

You do not need answers. You need to have thought about these things enough to have a real conversation. Your advisor has heard every version of “I’m not sure yet.” That is fine.

How do I know when I have enough saved?

There is no single number that works for everyone. What matters more is whether your guaranteed or predictable income — Social Security, a pension, or other steady sources — covers your essential expenses. The gap between that income and your needs is what your savings has to fill.

A useful starting point: Add up what you reliably expect each month (Social Security, pension, rental income). Then add up what you need to cover basic living costs. If there is a gap, that is what the rest of your plan is designed to address.

A retirement income review can help you map this out clearly. Many people find the gap is smaller than they feared — or that closing it is more straightforward than they expected, depending on their situation.

Should I worry about inflation running down my savings?

It is a fair concern. If your living costs rise over time but your income stays flat, your money buys less each year. This is often called purchasing-power risk, and it is one of the most common issues people in their 60s and 70s overlook.

Some income sources — like Social Security — include cost-of-living adjustments that may help over time. Others are fixed. Part of what a retirement income review looks at is how your income sources are likely to hold up over a long retirement, and whether adjustments may make sense depending on your situation.

What happens during the review itself?

The conversation is straightforward. There are no tests, no pressure, and nothing to sign at the first meeting. Here is a general picture of how it often goes:

  • You share your documents and talk through your situation. The advisor listens more than talks at this stage.
  • Together, you build a picture of your income and expenses. This includes what you have, what is coming in, and what you need.
  • You identify any gaps or risks worth addressing. This might be a shortfall, a tax issue, or a concern about what happens if one spouse lives much longer than the other.
  • Options are discussed, not pushed. A good advisor explains what is available and why certain approaches may or may not fit your situation. You decide what, if anything, to do next.

You are allowed to say: “I need time to think about that.” A trustworthy advisor will not rush you.

What if I do not have everything organized yet?

Most people do not. Bring what you have. A partial picture is better than no picture. If you have lost track of an old 401(k), forgotten about a small pension, or simply never looked at your Social Security statement, the review process often helps you locate and organize those things.

The goal is not perfection. The goal is to start the conversation with enough information to see where you actually stand.

If you have been putting off taking stock of your retirement picture, a retirement income review is a calm, no-pressure way to start. Gather the documents listed above, jot down a few of the questions that have been on your mind, and come ready to have an honest conversation. There is no obligation at the first meeting — just a clearer sense of where you stand and what, if anything, may need attention. Reaching out to schedule a retirement income review is often the step that turns years of worry into a workable plan.
