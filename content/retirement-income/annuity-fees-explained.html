If someone handed you a contract with terms like “mortality and expense charge,” “surrender period,” or “participation rate,” you might set it right back down. Those words sound complicated on purpose. But the ideas behind them are not. This page walks through each one the way you might talk about it over coffee.

What is the fee called a “mortality and expense charge” on a variable annuity?

A variable annuity puts your money into investment accounts that go up and down with the market. Because the insurance company is taking on certain risks—like promising to pay your heirs a minimum death benefit, for example—it charges an annual fee to cover those risks. That fee is called the mortality and expense (M&E) charge.

It is usually expressed as a percentage of your account value each year. A typical range is roughly 1% to 1.5% per year, though it varies by contract. The fee comes out of your account automatically; you will not write a separate check. It is important to understand, though, that the fee is charged whether the market goes up or down.

Plain-English version: Think of M&E as the cost of the insurance wrapper around your investment accounts. You pay it every year as long as the contract is in force.

What are rider fees and what do they pay for?

Many annuity contracts let you add optional features called riders. A rider is essentially an extra promise from the insurance company—for an extra cost.

Common riders you may encounter include:

  • Income rider (guaranteed lifetime withdrawal benefit): This rider may guarantee that you can take a set percentage out each year for life, even if your account balance drops to zero. Fees for this type of rider often run between 0.5% and 1.25% per year.
  • Long-term care rider: Some contracts allow you to take out more money—or double your withdrawal—if you are confined to a care facility or need in-home nursing help. This rider typically adds another 0.25% to 0.75% per year, depending on the contract.
  • Enhanced death benefit rider: Promises your heirs a minimum payout regardless of market performance at the time of your death. Cost varies widely.

Rider fees are deducted from your account value each year in addition to the base M&E charge. Before adding a rider, it helps to ask: Will I actually need this feature, and does the cost make sense for my situation?

What is a surrender charge and how long does it last?

When you put money into most annuities, the insurance company invests it in longer-term assets to support its guarantees. Because of this, they charge a penalty—called a surrender charge—if you take out more than a certain amount in the early years of the contract.

Surrender periods typically run 5 to 10 years. The charge usually starts higher and steps down each year until it reaches zero. Here is how a common 7-year schedule might look:

Most contracts also include a free withdrawal provision—typically 10% of your account value each year—that you can take without triggering the surrender charge. Amounts above that threshold during the surrender period will be reduced by the applicable charge.

Worth knowing: If you need access to a large sum in the first few years, a surrender charge could reduce the amount you receive. Make sure you have other liquid savings set aside before committing money to an annuity.

How do caps and participation rates work on a fixed indexed annuity?

A fixed indexed annuity (FIA) is different from a variable annuity. Your money is not directly invested in the market. Instead, the insurance company credits interest based on how a market index—such as the S&P 500—performs over a set period. Two terms govern how much of that index gain you actually receive:

Cap rate

A cap is the highest amount of interest the contract will credit in a given period, regardless of how much the index actually rose. If the cap is 8% and the index gains 14%, you receive 8%. If the index gains 5%, you receive 5% (up to the cap).

Participation rate

A participation rate tells you what percentage of the index gain is credited to your account. A 70% participation rate means if the index rises 10%, you are credited 7%. Some contracts use a cap and a participation rate together; others use just one.

In exchange for accepting these limits on upside, the contract typically protects you from market losses—your account will not go below a certain floor (often 0%) due to index declines. That protection is a core part of what you are paying for.

What is a spread fee and how does it reduce my credited interest?

Some fixed indexed annuities use a spread instead of—or in addition to—a cap or participation rate. A spread is a percentage that is subtracted from the index gain before interest is credited to you.

For example, if the index gains 9% and the contract has a 2% spread, you are credited 7%. The spread is effectively a built-in fee that the insurance company keeps as part of how it funds the contract’s guarantees and operating costs. It does not show up as a separate line item the way M&E charges do, but it reduces what you earn just the same.

What guarantees do annuities actually provide, and are they solid?

The word “guarantee” gets used a lot when annuities are discussed. Here is what it typically means in practice:

  • Guaranteed lifetime income: Certain contracts may guarantee a stream of income you cannot outlive, often through an income rider or by converting (annuitizing) the contract. The amount and conditions vary by contract and your age at the time.
  • Guaranteed minimum credited rate: Many fixed annuities promise a minimum interest rate floor—often 1% to 3%—even if market conditions are poor.
  • Principal protection on indexed products: FIAs typically guarantee that market losses will not reduce your account value below a set floor (often 0%), subject to the terms of the contract.

Important disclosure: All annuity guarantees—including lifetime income, minimum rates, and principal protection—are subject to the insurance company’s claims-paying ability. An annuity is only as solid as the company behind it. Checking an insurer’s financial strength ratings before purchasing is a reasonable step.

How do all these fees add up, and what should I ask before signing?

It helps to look at the total annual cost of a contract—not just one charge in isolation. Here is a rough picture of how fees can stack on a variable annuity with riders:

Before agreeing to any annuity contract, it is reasonable to ask:

  • What is the total annual cost, including every fee layer?
  • What is the surrender schedule, and how long does it last?
  • Can I take any money out penalty-free each year, and how much?
  • If I add a rider, what does it actually promise, and under what conditions does it pay?
  • What is the insurer’s financial strength rating?
  • Does the cap rate or participation rate change after the first year?

A good advisor should be able to walk through every one of these questions with patience and without pressure. If the answers feel rushed or unclear, that is worth paying attention to.
