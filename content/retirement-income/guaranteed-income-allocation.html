One of the most common worries we hear from people approaching retirement is simple: “What if I outlive my money?” The good news is there is a practical framework that can help you sleep better at night — and it does not require a crystal ball.

What does “guaranteed income” actually mean in retirement?

Guaranteed income is money you can count on arriving every month, no matter how the stock market is performing or how long you live. The most familiar examples are Social Security and a traditional pension. Some retirees also add income from a fixed annuity, which may provide similar predictability, though annuity guarantees are subject to the insurance company’s claims-paying ability.

The key word is predictable. You know it is coming. You can plan around it. That predictability is exactly what makes it valuable in retirement, when you no longer have a paycheck.

Worth knowing

“Guaranteed” does not mean risk-free in every sense. Social Security could face changes over time. Pensions depend on their plan’s financial health. And annuity income is backed by the insurer, not the federal government. Knowing the source of each guarantee matters.

What is the “income floor” and how does it work?

Retirement researchers often describe a two-layer approach sometimes called the income floor strategy. The idea is straightforward:

Flexible Layer Discretionary spending — travel, gifts, hobbies Funded by investments (stocks, bonds, savings). Can be dialed back in a down market without affecting your essentials. Income Floor Essential spending — housing, food, utilities, healthcare Covered by guaranteed sources: Social Security, pension, and/or annuity income. Does not depend on market performance. The income floor covers needs; the flexible layer covers wants.

The beauty of this setup is that a bad year in the stock market does not have to threaten your heat or your groceries. Your floor holds. You may choose to travel less that year, but the basics are covered.

How much of my retirement income should be guaranteed?

There is no single right number that works for everyone. However, a widely used starting point in retirement planning is this idea: your guaranteed income should cover at least your essential monthly expenses. Everything above that can come from investments.

Researchers and planners often discuss rough heuristics. These are not rules — they are starting points for a conversation:

Common heuristics for the share of income that may come from guaranteed sources
Your situation What planners often discuss
Essential expenses are close to what Social Security covers Your floor may already be in place; investments cover the rest
Essential expenses exceed guaranteed income by a meaningful gap Some explore adding a guaranteed source (e.g., a fixed annuity) to close the gap
You have significant investment assets and low fixed costs A higher share of discretionary spending may come from investments; less pressure on the floor
You have health concerns or a family history of longevity A larger floor may offer more peace of mind over a long retirement

These are general illustrations, not personalized advice. Your specific numbers will depend on your expenses, health, and other income sources.

A useful first step

Write down your essential monthly expenses — housing, utilities, food, medications, insurance. Then write down your guaranteed monthly income from Social Security and any pension. The difference, if any, is your “gap.” That gap is the number worth planning around.

Should I keep some money in investments even with a solid income floor?

Yes, in most situations. Here is why: a fully guaranteed portfolio may not keep up with inflation over a 20- or 30-year retirement. Prices go up over time, and a modest amount of investment growth can help your purchasing power hold steady.

Investments also give you flexibility. If a large expense comes up — a home repair, helping a family member, a medical cost — having accessible savings matters. The floor handles the predictable; investments handle the unexpected and the optional.

Keep in mind

The right balance between guaranteed income and investments depends on factors unique to you: your spending habits, health, other savings, whether you have a spouse or partner, and your comfort with uncertainty. No single formula applies to everyone.

How do I figure out if my current income floor is strong enough?

A simple way to start: pull together a few pieces of information.

  • Your expected Social Security benefit — you can find an estimate on the Social Security Administration website (ssa.gov).
  • Any pension income — check with your former employer or plan administrator.
  • Your essential monthly expenses today — a rough estimate is fine to start.

If your guaranteed income covers those essential expenses, you are in a strong starting position. If there is a gap, that does not mean something is wrong — it just means there is a planning question worth exploring.

What are the trade-offs of putting more money into guaranteed income?

Locking in more guaranteed income often means giving up some flexibility. For example, some guaranteed income sources are not easily accessible as a lump sum if an emergency arises. They trade liquidity for predictability.

That is not good or bad on its own. It depends on what you value more: the certainty of a steady check, or the ability to access your money quickly when needed. Most people find that some combination of both feels right.

  • More guaranteed income: greater peace of mind, less worry about market swings, may reduce growth potential
  • More in investments: greater flexibility and potential growth, more exposure to market ups and downs
  • A mix of both: what most retirement income plans aim for, depending on individual circumstances

Does the right balance change as I get older?

It often does. Early in retirement, when you may be more active and want flexibility, a larger share in investments can make sense. As you age, the predictability of guaranteed income often becomes more valuable — you may be less interested in managing investments, and more interested in simply knowing the money will arrive.

Some people choose to gradually shift their mix over time. Others set their floor early and leave the rest in a simple investment strategy. Neither approach is wrong; what matters is that you have thought it through with your specific situation in mind.

Thinking about your own income floor? A retirement income review can help you see where you stand — how much of your essential spending is already covered, whether there is a gap worth addressing, and what options may be available to you. There is no obligation and no pressure, just a clear look at your numbers so you can make an informed decision at your own pace.
