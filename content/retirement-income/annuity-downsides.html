Annuities are not good or bad on their own. Like most financial tools, they fit some situations well and others poorly. Before you consider one, it helps to know exactly what the drawbacks are.

What makes annuities hard to understand?

Annuities come in many forms. Fixed, variable, indexed, immediate, deferred — each works differently, and each has its own set of rules. The contracts themselves can run 30 pages or more, written in technical language that even experienced investors find confusing.

That complexity matters because you cannot make a good decision about something you do not fully understand. If a product is hard to explain in plain language, that is worth paying attention to.

How long can my money be locked up?

Most deferred annuities include a surrender period — a stretch of time, often 5 to 10 years, during which you cannot withdraw your money without paying a penalty called a surrender charge.

Those charges can start high (sometimes 7–10 percent) and step down gradually each year. If you need access to your funds during that window for a health event, home repair, or family need, the cost can be significant.

Important: Even after the surrender period ends, early withdrawals before age 59½ may also trigger a 10% IRS penalty on top of ordinary income taxes on any earnings.

What fees should I watch for?

Annuities can carry several layers of cost. Not every annuity has all of these, but it is worth asking about each one:

Fee Type What It Covers Typical Range
Mortality & expense charge Insurance cost built into variable annuities 0.5% – 1.5% per year
Administrative fee Record-keeping and contract maintenance $0 – $50 per year
Subaccount (fund) expenses Investment management inside variable products 0.5% – 2.0% per year
Rider fees Optional income, death-benefit, or care benefits 0.5% – 1.5% per year, each
Surrender charge Early exit penalty during surrender period 1% – 10%, depending on year

Fees compound quietly. A combination of charges adding up to 2 or 3 percent per year can meaningfully reduce how much your money grows over time.

What happens if I need cash in an emergency?

This is one of the most practical concerns. Annuities are designed to hold money for the long term. Most contracts allow a small free-withdrawal amount each year (often 10 percent of the account value), but beyond that, you face surrender charges.

If a large portion of your savings is inside an annuity, you may have less flexibility to respond to unexpected expenses. Financial planners often describe this as liquidity risk — the risk of needing access to money that is not readily available.

A common guideline is to keep three to six months of living expenses in accessible accounts before committing funds to any long-term product.

Why is it hard to compare annuity prices?

Unlike a mutual fund or a CD, annuity pricing is not easy to compare from one company to the next. Contracts differ in features, riders, and terms, making apples-to-apples comparisons difficult. Insurers are not required to disclose their profit margins, and the internal cost structure of some products — particularly indexed annuities — can be especially opaque.

This matters because you may not know whether you are getting a fair deal or an expensive one without doing significant research, often with professional help.

Should I be concerned about how annuities are sold?

Most annuities are sold by licensed insurance agents who earn a commission when you buy. Commissions can be substantial — sometimes 5 to 8 percent or more of the amount you invest. That commission is not a line item on your statement; it is built into the product's structure.

This does not mean every agent has bad intentions. Many are genuinely trying to help. But it does mean you should ask directly: How are you compensated if I purchase this? A straightforward answer is a good sign. Hesitation or vague answers are worth noting.

  • Ask whether the person advising you is a fiduciary — legally required to put your interests first.
  • Request a written summary of all fees, charges, and the advisor's compensation before you sign anything.
  • Take your time. A reputable professional will not pressure you to decide on the spot.

Are annuity guarantees truly guaranteed?

Annuities often include guarantees — a promise that you will receive income for life, or that your account value will not fall below a certain amount. These can be valuable features. But they are not unconditional.

Any guarantee offered by an annuity is subject to the insurance company's claims-paying ability. If the insurer runs into serious financial trouble, state guaranty associations may provide a safety net, but coverage limits vary by state and may not cover the full value of your contract.

Does this mean annuities are always a bad idea?

No. The downsides above are real, but they do not make annuities wrong for every person. Some people have specific needs — a desire for predictable income they cannot outlive, or protection against a sharp market drop late in retirement — where certain annuity features may fit well, depending on their situation.

The key word is fit. A product that works for a neighbor or sibling may not be suitable for you. The right question is not whether annuities are good or bad in general, but whether a particular product addresses your specific goals, income needs, time horizon, and overall financial picture.

  • Complexity — Suits people who take the time to understand exactly what they are buying.
  • Illiquidity — Better suited to funds you genuinely will not need for many years.
  • Fees — More acceptable when the benefits (e.g., lifetime income) clearly outweigh the cost.
  • Guarantees — Most meaningful when purchased from a highly rated, financially stable insurer.
