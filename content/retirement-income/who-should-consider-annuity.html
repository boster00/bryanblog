An annuity is not right for everyone. It is a specific tool designed to solve a specific problem. Before anything else, the honest question to ask is whether it fits your situation — not someone else's.

What is the basic idea behind an annuity?

At its simplest, an annuity is a contract between you and an insurance company. You hand over a sum of money, and in return the insurer agrees to send you a regular payment — often for as long as you live. The appeal is straightforward: you cannot outlive the income, no matter how long you are here.

Of course, that guarantee comes with trade-offs, and those trade-offs are worth understanding clearly before you decide anything.

Who may be a good fit for this kind of income?

Certain situations tend to make a steady, guaranteed income stream more attractive. You may want to explore this option if several of the following describe you:

  • Longevity runs in your family. If your parents or grandparents lived well into their 80s or 90s, your savings need to stretch further than the averages suggest.
  • You want a predictable income floor. Knowing a set amount will arrive each month — regardless of what the market does — can reduce anxiety and make it easier to budget.
  • Market swings keep you up at night. If watching a portfolio drop 20 percent makes you want to sell everything, locking a portion into something stable may help you stay the course with the rest.
  • Your Social Security and pension leave a gap. If those guaranteed sources do not fully cover your essential expenses, an annuity may help close that gap.
  • You do not need this money to be immediately accessible. Annuity contracts often restrict early withdrawals or impose surrender charges in the early years. If this is money you will not need as a lump sum, that restriction matters less.

Who should probably look at other options?

An annuity is not the right choice in every situation. Consider stepping back if any of these apply to you:

  • You already have abundant guaranteed income. If Social Security, a pension, and other sources already cover your living expenses comfortably, adding more guaranteed income may not add much value.
  • You need full flexibility with your money. Life brings surprises — medical bills, helping a child, home repairs. If this pool of money might be needed quickly, tying it up in a long-term contract is risky.
  • Your health suggests a shorter retirement horizon. Annuities are often most efficient over a long period. If a serious health condition limits your life expectancy, the math may not work in your favor. A fee-only financial planner can help you think through this carefully.
  • You are comfortable managing market risk. If you have a long investing track record, steady nerves, and a well-diversified portfolio, keeping your money invested may produce more over time — though with less certainty.
  • You want to leave a large inheritance. Some annuity structures reduce or eliminate what passes to your heirs. If leaving assets to family is a top priority, this trade-off needs careful thought.

What are the main trade-offs I should understand?

Every financial tool gives something and takes something. Here is how the key trade-offs typically look, depending on your situation:

Factor With an annuity Without an annuity
Income certainty Payments are set and predictable Depends on portfolio performance and withdrawals
Longevity risk Payments often continue for life Savings could run low if you live a long time
Flexibility Generally limited, especially early on Full access to funds at any time
Growth potential Varies by type; often more limited May grow more over time, with more risk
Inheritance May be reduced depending on structure Remaining balance passes to heirs

Should I put all my savings into one?

Almost certainly not. Most financial professionals suggest using an annuity for a specific purpose — covering essential expenses that Social Security and other guaranteed income do not fully cover — rather than putting everything into one contract.

Keeping a portion of savings in accessible, investable accounts gives you flexibility for unexpected costs and continued growth potential. The goal is to find the right balance between certainty and flexibility for your specific situation.

What else do I need to know before making a decision?

A few things are worth keeping in mind as you think this through:

  • Annuity income guarantees are subject to the insurance company's claims-paying ability. It is reasonable to ask about the insurer's financial strength ratings.
  • Fees and contract terms vary widely. Understanding exactly what you are buying — and what it costs — matters before signing anything.
  • Tax treatment depends on how the annuity is funded and how payments are taken. A tax professional can clarify what applies to your situation.
  • There is no urgency. A decision this significant deserves careful thought and, ideally, an independent second opinion.
A note on guarantees: Any income guarantee from an annuity is subject to the claims-paying ability of the issuing insurance company. Past performance of any financial product does not guarantee future results. Individual outcomes depend on contract terms, health, tax situation, and other personal factors.
