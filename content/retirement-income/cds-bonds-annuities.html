If you are trying to make your money last in retirement, you have probably heard of all three: certificates of deposit, bonds, and annuities. Each one can play a role. But they do different jobs, and mixing them up can lead to real surprises — like needing cash and finding it locked away.

What is each one actually doing for you?

Think of it this way. A CD is like putting money in a short-term vault. You know exactly what you will get back, and when. A bond is more like lending money to a company or government in exchange for regular interest payments. An annuity is an agreement with an insurance company: you give them a lump sum, and they agree to send you income — either for a set period or for as long as you live.

None of these is automatically better. The right question is: what job do I need done?

How do I know which one is safer?

Safety means different things depending on what worries you most.

  • CDs held at a bank are insured by the FDIC up to applicable limits. That is federal deposit insurance — your principal is protected if the bank fails.
  • Bonds are not FDIC-insured. A government bond is generally considered very low risk, while a corporate bond carries more. If the issuer runs into trouble, you may not get back everything you put in. Bond prices also move with interest rates, so if you need to sell before maturity, you could get less than you paid.
  • Annuity guarantees are backed by the issuing insurance company, not the federal government. Any income or return guarantees are subject to the insurance company’s claims-paying ability. Many states have guaranty funds that offer some protection, but coverage limits vary.

What if I need to get to my money quickly?

Liquidity — how easily you can access your money — varies a lot across these three.

  • CDs are fairly liquid if you are willing to pay an early-withdrawal penalty. Short-term CDs (3–12 months) give you access relatively soon.
  • Bonds can often be sold on the open market before they mature, but the price you get depends on current interest rates. You may get more or less than you originally paid.
  • Annuities typically have surrender periods — often several years — during which early withdrawals may trigger fees. Some annuities allow a limited annual withdrawal without penalty, but large or early withdrawals can be costly.

A practical rule of thumb: before putting money into any of these, make sure you have enough in a checking or savings account to cover 6–12 months of expenses. That way you are not forced to break a CD or sell a bond at the wrong time.

Should I worry about taxes on retirement income from these?

Yes, taxes are worth thinking about. The treatment differs.

  • CD interest is generally taxed as ordinary income in the year it is earned, even if you did not withdraw it.
  • Bond interest is also usually ordinary income, though interest from U.S. Treasury bonds is exempt from state and local tax, and some municipal bonds may be federally tax-exempt, depending on your situation.
  • Annuity earnings grow tax-deferred inside the contract. When you take income, the portion that represents growth is taxed as ordinary income. The portion that is a return of your original premium is generally not taxed again. Rules can be complex, so a tax professional’s guidance is worthwhile.

How predictable is the income from each one?

Predictability is often what retirees value most. Here is how the three compare.

  • CDs offer very predictable returns for their term — but when the term ends, you face reinvestment risk. If rates have dropped, your next CD may pay less.
  • Bonds pay a fixed coupon, so the interest is predictable. However, getting the full principal back requires holding to maturity or selling at a fair price — neither is guaranteed in practice.
  • Annuities can offer the most predictable income of all, especially those designed to pay a guaranteed monthly amount for life. This can help you cover fixed expenses no matter how long you live — though again, those guarantees are subject to the insurance company’s claims-paying ability.

How do I figure out the right mix for my situation?

Most people do not need to choose just one. A practical approach is to think about your money in layers.

  • Short-term layer: Cash and CDs for bills you know are coming in the next one to three years. Easy to reach, federally insured.
  • Middle layer: Bonds or bond funds for income in the three-to-ten year range, understanding that prices can fluctuate.
  • Long-term layer: If you are concerned about outliving your money, a portion in an annuity may help cover your essential monthly expenses for life — depending on your situation and health.

The right balance depends on factors like your other income sources, your health, how much you plan to leave to family, and your comfort with uncertainty.
