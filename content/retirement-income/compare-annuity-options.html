Thinking about a guaranteed income stream in retirement is smart planning. But not every annuity contract is the same, and not every agent will explain the differences clearly. Before you sign anything, here is a plain-language checklist you can bring to the table.

How do I know what questions to ask before buying?

Most people who later regret a purchase say the same thing: nobody walked me through what I was actually agreeing to. These ten questions are a starting point. A trustworthy advisor will welcome every one of them.

  • 1. What type of annuity is this, exactly? Fixed, fixed indexed, variable, or income? Each works differently. Make sure you understand which category you are looking at before comparing anything else.
  • 2. What is the guaranteed minimum I will receive, regardless of market performance or interest rates? Get this in writing, not just in a verbal summary.
  • 3. What are the surrender charges, and how long do they last? Many contracts lock up your money for 7–10 years. Find out what it would cost to get out early if your situation changes.
  • 4. What fees come out of my account each year? Ask for the total cost, including rider charges, mortality expenses, and any administrative fees. Small percentages add up over decades.
  • 5. Does this product include a lifetime income rider, and what does it cost? Riders can add real value, but they also add fees. Understand what you are paying for.
  • 6. How is the income calculated? Ask for a written example showing how your specific dollar amount would translate into monthly payments at different ages.
  • 7. What happens to the remaining balance when I die? Does money pass to your spouse or children, or does it stop? This matters a great deal for couples.
  • 8. Is there any inflation adjustment built in? A fixed payment that looks comfortable today may feel tight in 15 years if prices keep rising.
  • 9. What percentage of my savings am I putting into this? Concentrating too much of your nest egg in one product — even a safe one — can limit your flexibility.
  • 10. Who is the issuing insurance company, and how do I check their financial strength? You will be relying on this company to keep its promises for potentially 20–30 years.

What should I look for when reading the contract?

The contract is the only document that legally matters. Sales brochures, illustrations, and verbal explanations are helpful, but the contract controls. Look closely at these areas before you sign.

  • The "free look" period. Most states require a window (often 10–30 days) during which you can return the contract for a full refund, no questions asked. Know your deadline and use the time.
  • Definitions of key terms. Words like "accumulation value," "surrender value," and "income base" may not mean what you expect. Read each definition section carefully or ask the advisor to walk through them with you line by line.
  • Participation rates and caps (for indexed products). These determine how much of any market gain you actually receive. They can also be adjusted over time. Find out what the minimum guaranteed rate and cap are — not just today's figures.
  • Rider terms and activation rules. Some income riders require you to wait a set number of years before you can start drawing. Others have "deferral bonus" language that sounds attractive but only applies to the income calculation, not the actual account value you could withdraw.
  • Exclusions and limitations. Look for conditions that could reduce or suspend payments — for example, failing to meet withdrawal timing requirements or triggering certain penalty clauses.

A practical tip: If any section of the contract is unclear, ask the advisor to put the explanation in writing and attach it to your file. Vague verbal reassurances are not binding.

Why does the insurance company's financial strength matter?

An annuity guarantee is only as reliable as the company standing behind it. Unlike a bank account, annuity assets are not federally insured. Your protection comes from two places: the financial health of the issuing company, and your state's guaranty fund (which has limits and varies by state).

Independent rating agencies evaluate insurance companies and publish letter-grade ratings that reflect the company's ability to meet its long-term financial obligations. These ratings are updated regularly, and the methodology looks at capital reserves, investment quality, and claims history, among other factors.

Rating Tier What It Generally Signals What to Consider
Superior / Excellent Very strong capacity to meet policyholder obligations A reasonable starting point for long-term commitments
Good / Adequate Sufficient capacity, but more sensitive to economic shifts Worth additional scrutiny; monitor over time
Below Investment Grade Elevated risk of difficulty meeting future obligations Use extra caution; seek a second opinion

All guarantees described in an annuity contract are subject to the insurance company's claims-paying ability. A company's rating can change over the years, so it is reasonable to check it again periodically after purchase, not just at the time you buy.

Should I get a second opinion before I sign?

Yes — and this may be the most valuable step of all.

The agent presenting a product to you is typically compensated by commission when you buy. That does not make them dishonest, but it does mean their perspective is shaped by the products they sell. A second opinion from an advisor who is not selling the product — and who is paid by you rather than by commission — gives you a cleaner read on whether the product fits your situation.

Here is what a non-selling second opinion can offer:

  • An independent look at whether the product matches your income needs, timeline, and health situation.
  • A fee comparison against other ways to create retirement income.
  • A plain-language translation of contract language you found confusing.
  • An honest assessment of how much of your total savings is reasonable to commit to a single contract.

You are not obligated to act on the second opinion — but having it often either confirms you are making a solid decision or surfaces something worth reconsidering before the free-look window closes.

How do I compare one annuity to another side by side?

Comparing illustrations from two different products can be genuinely confusing because companies present numbers differently. A few principles help cut through the noise.

  • Use the same starting dollar amount for both illustrations so the comparison is apples-to-apples.
  • Ask for a "worst case" scenario, not just the projected or mid-point scenario. How does each product perform if market conditions are poor or interest rates stay low?
  • Compare total income received at age 80, 85, and 90 — not just the annual payment figure. A higher annual payment sometimes comes with a shorter guaranteed period.
  • Look at what heirs would receive under both contracts at the same assumed ages. Death benefit provisions differ widely.
  • Factor in the total fees over a 10-year period. A lower headline income payment with lower fees may outperform a flashier illustration over time.

Many states require agents to give you a standardized "buyer's guide" before you purchase. If you have not received one, ask for it. It is free and written in plain language.

What are common mistakes people make when buying?

Knowing where others have gone wrong can help you avoid the same missteps. These come up often in retirement planning conversations.

  • Buying without reading the contract. The illustration is a sales tool. The contract is the agreement. They are not always perfectly aligned.
  • Putting too large a share of savings into one product. Liquidity still matters in retirement. Unexpected medical costs or home repairs do not wait for surrender charges to expire.
  • Confusing the income base with the account value. These are often two different numbers. Only one can be withdrawn as cash.
  • Assuming all guarantees are equal. The word "guaranteed" appears frequently in annuity marketing. The weight of that guarantee depends on the specific contract terms and the financial strength of the issuing company.
  • Waiting too long for the "perfect moment." Rates and terms change. Staying in cash indefinitely while waiting for better conditions has its own cost — lost purchasing power and delayed income security.
