Will my money last as long as I do?

That question — plain and honest — is the one that matters most. And there is a reason it feels so hard to answer: retirement income is not like a paycheck. A paycheck shows up every two weeks no matter what the stock market is doing. The money you have saved does not come with that promise attached.

How long might retirement actually last?

Many financial plans from a generation ago assumed retirement would last 10 to 15 years. Today, the math looks different. A 65-year-old man in average health has roughly a 50% chance of living past age 85. A 65-year-old woman has roughly a 50% chance of living past age 87. If you are a couple, there is a better-than-even chance one of you will still be alive at 90.

That is not cause for alarm — it is cause for planning. A plan that runs out of money at 82 when you live to 93 is not a success. Longevity is the foundation of every retirement income conversation worth having.

Social Security covers part of the picture — but rarely all of it

Social Security is the most dependable income most retirees have. It is inflation-adjusted, it lasts as long as you live, and it does not depend on investment performance. But for most households it was never designed to replace a full working income. The average monthly benefit as of recent years is well under two thousand dollars. For many families, that covers basic expenses — housing, food, utilities — but leaves a meaningful gap when you add in healthcare, transportation, travel, and the unexpected.

A useful starting point: Add up your essential monthly expenses — the ones that show up whether you leave the house or not. Then subtract your guaranteed monthly income (Social Security, pension, any other income that cannot disappear). The difference is your income gap — the amount your savings need to cover every single month, for as long as you live.

Estimating your gap

You do not need a spreadsheet to get a rough number. Take your estimated monthly expenses in retirement. Subtract what you expect from Social Security and any pension. If the number is positive, that monthly shortfall is what your savings need to fill. Multiply it by 12 and you can see the annual draw you are looking at. Multiply that by 20 or 25 years and the total can feel large — which is exactly why how you draw that money matters just as much as how much you have saved.

The goal of the rest of this guide is to show you what tools exist to fill that gap reliably, how they compare, and how to think through which ones make sense for your specific situation.

What’s a simple plan for steady income in retirement?

The old model was simple: retire, live off your savings. The problem is that a single pool of money doing all the work at once is exposed to everything at once — market drops, big expenses, long life. A layered approach separates your income into sources that each do a specific job, so no single thing carries all the weight.

Think of it as replacing a paycheck with multiple layers

When you worked, your paycheck was predictable. You knew roughly what was coming in each month. In retirement, the goal is to rebuild that predictability from pieces — some guaranteed, some flexible, some growing slowly over time.

Why layering matters

Each layer has a different job. The first two — Social Security and pensions — cover the floor. Cash covers short-term needs. The middle layers generate income without requiring you to sell stocks. The investment portfolio grows over time but should not be the only thing standing between you and your monthly bills.

The mix that is right for you depends on your gap, your savings, your risk comfort, and how much flexibility you want. There is no single formula. But understanding what each layer does makes the choices that follow much clearer.

How much should I keep safe versus invested?

This is one of the most important questions in retirement planning, and it is also one of the most misunderstood. The answer is not simply “keep more in cash if you are nervous” or “stay invested so you do not miss growth.” It depends on timing, and timing matters in retirement in a way it never did while you were saving.

The danger of a big market drop early in retirement

When you are still working and the market falls sharply, you can wait. You keep contributing, prices recover over time, and you make up the losses. But once you are retired and drawing money out each month, a sharp market drop in the first few years can do lasting damage to your plan — damage that does not fully repair itself even if the market eventually recovers. This is because you are selling shares at low prices to cover living expenses, which means fewer shares available when prices rise again.

This is why having a portion of your money in stable, predictable sources is not just about comfort. It is about protecting your ability to let the rest keep growing. You do not want to be forced to sell investments at the worst time simply because you need grocery money.

Finding your balance

There is no universal right answer for the split between stable and invested money. Some people feel comfortable with most of their savings in the market, as long as they have two or three years of expenses covered by stable sources. Others want a larger portion that is simply guaranteed, so they can stop watching the market. The goal is to build a plan where a bad year in the stock market does not threaten your ability to pay the bills — without giving up so much growth potential that inflation erodes your purchasing power over time.

A practical rule of thumb: Many advisors suggest keeping at least one to three years of “gap income” — the amount your savings need to cover each month, beyond Social Security and any pension — in stable, accessible sources. This gives your investments time to recover if markets fall without forcing you to sell at a loss. Your situation may call for more or less, depending on your total picture.

What do I give up to get more certainty?

Certainty in retirement income is real and valuable — but it is not free. Before you move money toward any guaranteed-income product, it is worth being clear-eyed about what you are trading away. This section takes annuity skepticism seriously, because that skepticism is well-founded in some cases and important to address honestly.

What annuities actually are

An annuity is a contract with an insurance company. You give the company a sum of money, and in exchange the company promises to pay you income — either for a fixed period or, in the case of lifetime income products, for as long as you live. Like any contract, the specific terms matter enormously. Different types of annuities work very differently, and the word “annuity” covers a wide range of products with very different costs, structures, and trade-offs.

What you give up

The primary trade-off is flexibility for certainty. When you put money into most annuity contracts, you are agreeing to leave it there — often for a period of years called the surrender period — or face a penalty to take it out early. That is the cost of the insurance company being able to make you a long-term promise. Here is a plain breakdown of what to watch for:

  • Surrender charges: Early withdrawals during the surrender period — often 5 to 10 years — typically come with a penalty that declines over time. This is not hidden, but it is important to understand before you commit.
  • Fees: Some annuities, particularly variable annuities or annuities with optional riders, carry annual fees. These can be meaningful and reduce the net income or accumulation value over time. Fixed and fixed indexed annuities typically have lower or no explicit fees, though the cost is built into the contract terms.
  • Complexity: Some contracts are genuinely hard to read. Terms like “cap rate,” “participation rate,” “spread,” “benefit base,” and “roll-up rate” are not intuitive. You should not sign anything you cannot explain in plain English.
  • Liquidity: Unlike a CD where the penalty is modest and the term is short, annuity surrender periods can be long. If you need the money for a major unexpected expense, getting it out may cost you. Most contracts do include a provision for withdrawing a small percentage — often 10% — each year without penalty, but the rest is locked up.
  • Opacity: It can be difficult to comparison-shop annuities the way you would compare CD rates, because the terms are more complex and the products are not standardized. This is a legitimate concern and one reason that getting a second opinion is always a good idea.

What you may gain

In exchange for those trade-offs, annuities — when they are the right fit — can do things that other financial products cannot:

  • Guaranteed income for life: An income annuity or annuity with a lifetime income rider may pay you a set amount for as long as you live, regardless of how long that is. No other non-government financial product offers this feature, subject to the insurance company’s claims-paying ability.
  • Protection from market loss: Fixed and fixed indexed annuities do not lose value due to market downturns. Your principal is protected within the terms of the contract.
  • Predictability: Knowing a certain amount is coming in each month, regardless of what the market does, can reduce anxiety and allow the rest of your money to be invested with a longer horizon.

Other features worth understanding

Some annuity contracts include optional features that may matter depending on your situation:

  • Inflation protection: Some contracts offer payments that rise over time. This often means a lower starting payment, so the trade-off is real. Not all products offer this feature.
  • Death benefits: Some contracts pay a benefit to your heirs if you die before receiving back what you put in. Terms vary widely.
  • Long-term care provisions: Some contracts allow you to access more income if you need long-term care. These features add complexity and cost, and should be evaluated carefully.
  • Insurance company strength: Because annuity guarantees depend on the company’s ability to pay, the financial strength ratings of the insurer matter. State guaranty associations provide some protection, but they are not unlimited and they vary by state.

The honest bottom line: More certainty costs flexibility. That trade-off may be well worth it for some of your money, and poorly suited for other parts. The goal is not to maximize certainty or maximize flexibility — it is to build a plan where the certain part covers what must be covered, and the flexible part handles everything else.

How do I know whether an annuity actually fits my situation?

The most useful way to think about this is not “should I buy an annuity?” That question leads to yes-or-no thinking that is rarely helpful. A better question is: what job does this money need to do? Once you answer that, the right tool becomes much easier to identify.

The “job-to-be-done” framework

Different portions of your savings may need to do different things. Some money needs to be completely accessible for emergencies. Some needs to generate income starting now. Some needs to grow over the next decade to offset inflation. Some may be earmarked for a specific purpose — a home repair, travel, leaving something to family. An annuity is not a good fit for all of these jobs. It may be an excellent fit for one or two.

May be a good fit if…

  • You have a meaningful income gap after Social Security and any pension
  • You are anxious about outliving your money and that anxiety is affecting your quality of life
  • You have money that is not needed for near-term emergencies
  • You want a portion of your income to be predictable regardless of markets
  • You are in reasonable health and may live a long time
  • You can afford to leave the money in place during the surrender period

May not be a good fit if…

  • You need the money to be fully accessible within the next few years
  • Your income gap is already covered by Social Security, a pension, or other guaranteed sources
  • You have significant debt that may require a lump-sum payoff
  • You are in poor health and may not benefit from lifetime payments
  • The money earmarked is intended as a near-term inheritance
  • You have not yet built a basic emergency fund outside of retirement accounts

There is no one-size answer

Many people who are right for an annuity do not put all of their money into one. They may use one portion of their savings to secure a baseline income they cannot outlive, while keeping the rest invested for growth and flexibility. Others find that Social Security and a pension already cover their essential needs, and an annuity does not add enough to justify the trade-offs. Both conclusions can be correct depending on the person.

The goal is to match the right tool to the right job — not to assume that any product is universally good or bad.

How do I compare options without getting lost in the fine print?

Once you move from education to evaluation — meaning you are actually looking at specific options, not just understanding concepts — the process can feel overwhelming fast. There are dozens of products, varying terms, and sales materials that all make things sound straightforward. The trick is to organize your evaluation by what you need, not by product label or marketing language.

Start with the job, not the product name

Instead of asking “should I get a fixed indexed annuity or a bond ladder?” ask “what am I trying to accomplish with this portion of my money?” If the job is “produce guaranteed income I cannot outlive,” only a small number of products actually do that. If the job is “earn more than a savings account without losing principal,” that is a different list. If the job is “grow over the next decade to keep up with inflation,” that is a third list. Organizing by need narrows the comparison to relevant options.

Questions to ask before agreeing to anything

Whether you are evaluating an annuity, a CD, a bond ladder, or any other income-generating option, these questions help cut through complexity:

  1. What is the guaranteed income amount, and what is projected but not guaranteed? (These are two different numbers and should be shown separately.)
  2. When does income start, and can I choose to delay it?
  3. If I need money unexpectedly, how much can I access, and what does it cost me?
  4. What fees am I paying, and how are they charged? (Explicitly stated, or built into the rate?)
  5. How long is the surrender period, and what are the penalties in each year?
  6. Does the income amount change over time? Is it fixed, or does it rise with some measure of inflation?
  7. What happens to the payments if I die earlier than expected? Does anything go to a spouse or heirs?
  8. What is the financial strength rating of the company offering this product?
  9. Is there a long-term care feature, and if so, what triggers it and how much does it add or cost?
  10. How does this option compare to simply delaying Social Security if I have not yet claimed?

Comparing income options side by side

When you are looking at two or more options, try to put them in a table with the same columns. It is easy for one proposal to look better simply because it emphasizes different information. If a salesperson or advisor is not willing to show you the comparison in a clear, side-by-side format, that is worth noting.

A few specific things to watch out for:

  • Guaranteed vs. projected income: Make sure the income number you are being shown is guaranteed, not just the projected amount based on optimistic assumptions.
  • Benefit base vs. actual account value: Some products show a large “benefit base” number that looks like your account value but is not money you can access in a lump sum. It is only used to calculate your income payments. These are not the same thing.
  • Bonus offers: Some contracts offer an upfront “premium bonus.” Bonuses are often offset by lower interest rates or longer surrender periods. They are rarely free money.

A practical step: Before any meeting with a financial professional, write down the one or two specific jobs you need your money to do. Bring that list. Ask them to show you the two or three options that best fit those specific jobs, with the trade-offs written out plainly. This keeps the conversation focused on your needs rather than on product features.

What should I do before making a decision?

Before you change anything about how your money is structured — before you move funds, sign a contract, or reallocate investments — there is a step that is more valuable than any product: a clear picture of where you stand. A retirement income review is not a sales pitch. It is an organized look at your income sources, your expenses, your assets, and your goals. It gives you a baseline from which any decision can be evaluated honestly.

What a retirement income review covers

A thorough review looks at your full picture, not just your investments. It maps your income sources against your estimated expenses, identifies gaps, and flags areas of risk — including the risk of a market drop at the wrong time, the risk of outliving your money, and the risk of healthcare costs that exceed what you have planned for.

The output is not a product recommendation. It is a map: here is where you are, here is what you need, here is the gap, and here are the general types of tools that can address it. From that map, specific product options can be evaluated — but only after the map is clear.

What to bring to a retirement income review

The more prepared you are, the more useful the conversation will be. Gathering this information before you sit down — or before you work through it on your own — makes the analysis far more accurate:

Monthly Expense Estimate Housing, food, utilities, healthcare, transportation, and discretionary spending. What to expect from the review

A good retirement income review does not end with a product sale. It ends with a clear picture: here is your estimated income gap, here are the tools that could address it, here are the trade-offs of each, and here is a suggested starting point. You should leave with enough information to make a thoughtful decision — not a pressured one.

If you have been putting this off because you are not sure where to start, a retirement income review is the right first step — not a sales appointment, not a product comparison, but a clear-eyed look at your numbers and your options. A conversation focused specifically on your retirement income can help you see exactly where you stand, identify the gap you are actually working with, and weigh the options that make sense for your situation — including whether any of them involve an annuity, and if so, which type and how much. There is no pressure in that conversation and no obligation to do anything right away. The goal is simply to replace uncertainty with a plan you can understand and feel confident about.

Reach out to schedule a retirement income review. Bring the information listed above, come with your questions, and expect a plain-spoken conversation — the kind you would have at the kitchen table with someone who is genuinely trying to help.
