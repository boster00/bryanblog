Most people who are five to ten years from retirement have a general sense of what they have saved. What is harder to see is whether those savings will actually cover monthly expenses once a paycheck stops. That gap—between what you expect to spend and what reliable income you have lined up—is exactly what a retirement income review looks at.

When does it make sense to reach out?

You do not need to have everything figured out first. A review is most useful when you are close enough to retirement to have real numbers, but still have time to adjust. A few situations where it tends to help:

  • You are within five to ten years of your planned retirement date
  • You are unsure how long your savings will last
  • You have had a change in income, health, or family situation recently
  • You are trying to decide when to claim Social Security
  • You want a second set of eyes before making a big financial decision

It is also fine to reach out simply because you have questions. There is no minimum account balance or level of preparation required. The conversation is meant to help you think, not to push you toward any particular product or decision.

What happens in a retirement income review?

Think of it as a working conversation, not a presentation. You share what you know about your expected income and expenses. The advisor helps you map out where the gaps are and what options exist to fill them. No decisions are required—the goal is to give you a clearer picture so you can make better choices on your own timeline.

A typical review covers a few key areas:

  • Your expected monthly expenses in retirement
  • Guaranteed income sources: Social Security, pensions, annuities you already own
  • How much your accounts may need to cover and for how long
  • Basic tradeoffs—for example, claiming Social Security earlier vs. later

What to bring to the conversation

You do not need a stack of paperwork. A rough idea of the numbers below is enough to make the conversation useful:

  • Your monthly living expenses—a ballpark is fine
  • A recent Social Security statement or estimate from ssa.gov
  • Account balances for your 401(k), IRA, or other savings
  • Any pension benefit summary you have received
  • A rough sense of when you want to stop working
